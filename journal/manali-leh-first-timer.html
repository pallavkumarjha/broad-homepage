What I got wrong on my first Manali-Leh.

ByFrom the Workshop DateOct 12, 2024 Read9 min WhereSarchu — Pang — Leh

I thought nine days was generous. I thought my Royal Enfield Himalayan was overkill. I thought I knew what altitude felt like because I had hiked Triund once. By the third pass, the road had disabused me of all three. This is the accounting.

We were a group of five. Two of us had ridden in the mountains before — by which I mean we had done Manali-Rohtang and turned around because the weather had turned. The other three were strong riders on roads that had been graded inside the last century. We had a WhatsApp group called "Leh or bust 2024" and a shared Google Sheet of waypoints which fell out of sync the moment the first rider lost signal at Atal Tunnel.

What follows are the four mistakes that nearly ended the ride, in the order they happened.

— 01

The altitude isn't a feeling. It's a deadline.

You read about AMS — Acute Mountain Sickness — in every guide. You skim it. You think: I am young, I am fit, I have done a yoga retreat in Rishikesh. You are wrong. AMS is not a measure of fitness. It is a measure of how fast you went up. I climbed from Manali (2,000 m) to Sarchu (4,290 m) in two and a half days, which everyone tells you is too fast and which I told myself would be fine.

It was not fine. At Sarchu I could not finish a roti. I could not sleep lying down. By 2 a.m. I was sitting up in a damp parachute tent, breathing through my nose like I was trying not to wake someone, except the someone was me.

The thing nobody quite tells you is that AMS is a deadline, not a sensation. Once it starts, the only correct response is to descend. Not "drink more water." Not "have some Diamox." Descend. I did not. I rode up to Tanglang La (5,328 m) the next morning because the group had to keep moving and I was too proud to be the reason we didn't.

I made it. The next time, I would not.

— 02

"I'll figure it out" is a plan that fails at 4,500 metres.

Our fuel strategy was: we will probably find pumps. This is a strategy that works on the Mumbai-Pune Expressway and approximately nowhere between Tandi and Karu. The stretch is 365 km. There is one functional pump at Sissu, an unreliable one at Pang (sometimes black-market only, sometimes nothing), and otherwise nothing.

The Himalayan does about 30 km to a litre at altitude. Tank is 15 litres. Math says you can do 450 km on a full tank, except you can't, because at 5,000 m the engine drinks more, the wind eats your range, and you absolutely do not want to be running on fumes coming over a pass.

I left Tandi with a full tank and ran out of usable fuel — the kind of usable where you stop being calm — about 12 km before Pang. I made it on faith. The faith was not deserved. We bought ten litres at twice the rate from a man with a plastic jerrycan whose name I do not remember and whose face I will not forget.

Bring extra fuel. Bring it before you need it.

— 03

The convoy gap that almost ended it.

This is the part I think about most. Our group of five had agreed, before leaving Manali, that we would ride staggered with a 100 m spread, and that the lead and tail would never be more than five minutes apart. We held this agreement for about four hours.

By the third day, the strongest rider — call him D — was twenty minutes ahead. The slowest, an old friend whose name I will not use here, was forty minutes behind. None of us could see each other. The middle three were strung out across the Gata Loops like a broken necklace.

It is conceptually obvious why this is bad. Concretely, what happens is: somebody falls. Or somebody runs out of fuel. Or somebody's chain snaps. Or — in our case — somebody pulls over to throw up because the altitude has finally caught up, and there is no way to tell anyone, because there is no signal between Sarchu and Pang for six hours of riding.

We figured it out by sitting on a rock at Pang for an hour and a half, waiting. He arrived. He was fine. We had no way of knowing he was fine. The hour and a half is the part I still think about.

If we had been able to see each other on a shared map, with last-known location and speed, the worry would have been a fact. The fact would have been: he is two switchbacks back, moving slowly, fine. Worry is a tax on attention you can't afford at altitude. We paid it for an hour and a half.

— 04

The papers I almost didn't have.

At the Lachung La checkpost, the constable wanted my driving licence and my registration certificate. I had both. I also had them in a clear plastic file in my saddlebag, which had spent the morning soaking up the runoff from a stream crossing. The licence was legible. The RC was a soggy abstract painting of itself.

He let me through. He did not have to. He looked at me the way you'd look at someone who had brought a paper map of Mumbai to drive in fog. The next checkpost did not let me through until I'd called the rental company and had them email a digital copy. That took thirty minutes. We were already running late on the schedule we did not have.

What I needed was: every document I owned, encrypted on my phone, available offline, accessible without unlocking. What I had was: paper.

This is, embarrassingly, one of the four reasons we built Broad's Glovebox. The other three were also embarrassing.

— 05

What I'd do differently.

Take an extra day. The whole route is engineered to be done in nine days because that's what fits in two weekends and a week of leave, but ten or eleven is a different ride. The body adapts on the rest day. The crew adapts on the rest day. The bike's fluids equilibrate on the rest day. I have never met a rider who regretted an extra day in Leh. I have met many who regretted skipping it.

Pre-load the offline map. The instinct is to figure out connectivity on the road. The reality is that connectivity figures out you. We use Broad now — full disclosure, we built it — but any offline-capable map app is better than none.

Carry an extra ten litres. The jerrycan looks ridiculous. You will not use it most days. The day you need it, you will need it absolutely.

Set a convoy spread. Five minutes between lead and tail. Enforce it. The strongest rider is not your benchmark. The slowest rider is your floor.

Don't be too proud to descend. The summit will be there next year. The rider who pushed through AMS in 2017 — and there are several — is also no longer riding. Make the trade.

Ladakh doesn't punish you for being unprepared. It just stops cooperating. The road is older than the country and is happy to be there next year. Ride it twice. Ride it slowly. Ride it like you know what you don't know.
