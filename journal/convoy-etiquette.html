12 rules of group riding (most crews learn the hard way).

ByFrom the Workshop DateNov 4, 2024 Read7 min ForCrews of 3 to 24

Group rides fall apart in patterns. After three years and roughly forty multi-day rides — most of them across India, some of them across continents — here is the list we wish someone had taped to a tank bag in 2021. Read it once, agree on it as a group, and then ride.

01

Set the slowest rider's pace, not the strongest's.

The strongest rider becomes the front of the convoy because they want to. The slowest rider becomes the floor of the convoy because the laws of physics demand it. The group's actual pace is the slowest rider's pace, whether you accept this or not.

Accept it openly. Tell the strongest rider to ride sweep for the first day. They will hate it for the first hour and learn something about the group by the second.

02

Ride staggered, not single file.

Single file looks pretty. Single file also means the rider behind you cannot see what is on the road in front of you, which is the entire point of riding behind you. Stagger — left wheel, right wheel, left wheel — and everyone gets a clear sightline.

The exceptions: hairpins, narrow ghats, and overtaking. Then you go single file. Then you go back to staggered.

03

Agree on maximum spread before you start.

Five minutes between lead and tail. Pick a number, write it down, enforce it. The lead rider's job is not to get to the destination first. It is to keep the group together.

If lead loses sight of the rider behind for more than thirty seconds, lead pulls over. Always. This is non-negotiable. The group that doesn't enforce this becomes two groups by lunch.

04

Hand signals over intercoms.

Bluetooth helmet intercoms are wonderful. They are also unreliable, battery-bound, and they break. Hand signals never run out of battery.

Agree on five before you leave: slow down, fuel stop, problem-pull-over, follow me, single file. Test them at the first traffic light.

05

The fuel rule: nobody runs below quarter.

If anyone in the convoy hits a quarter tank, the convoy stops at the next pump. Not the cheapest pump. Not the pump after the next ghat. The next pump.

This sounds excessive. It is not. The cost of a single fuel stop is twelve minutes. The cost of one rider running dry is everyone backtracking forty kilometres on a road they didn't want to ride twice.

06

Pick a tail rider and pay them for the role.

Not literally — though if you can, do. The tail rider's job is the most important and the least appreciated: they ride at the pace of the slowest member of the convoy and confirm by sight that nobody has fallen behind. They never overtake. They never lead.

Rotate the tail role daily. Anyone who has not ridden tail does not understand what it costs.

07

The convoy waits, or it isn't a convoy.

Someone needs to pee. Someone's strap is loose. Someone wants a photo. The convoy waits. Three minutes is fine. Ten minutes is fine. The minute the group decides "they can catch up," it stops being a group.

This is the rule that fails the most. It also fails first. Watch for it.

08

One route owner. Not five.

Group navigation by committee fails everywhere except the metro. One person owns the route. They have it loaded offline. They have the day's plan. They make the calls — fuel stops, lunch, bypasses, weather pivots.

The other riders advise. They do not vote. Voting on a switchback is how you miss the switchback.

09

Share everyone's location live.

Not "I'll text when I get there." Not "ping me if you need anything." Live. Visible. Refreshing. The point of a convoy is that the group is one organism, and the group cannot act like one organism if half of it is invisible to the other half.

This is what we built Broad for, frankly. Use whatever works — Google Maps location share, Apple's Find My, Broad's convoy view — but use something. Worry is a tax you don't have to pay.

10

An emergency contact list on every phone.

Every rider's emergency contact, blood group, and allergies on every other rider's phone. Before you leave. Not "I'll send it later." Before.

This sounds morbid. It is morbid. The reason it's a rule is that the few times we've needed it, we have always needed it without warning, in a place where we couldn't ask the rider in question.

11

The helmet-off rule.

If a rider takes their helmet off mid-ride, the group stops. Always. They might be hot. They might also be vomiting from altitude, hiding a bee sting, or about to faint. You don't know until you ask.

This rule has caught us once. Once is enough.

12

Debrief. Every night.

Twenty minutes over dinner. What worked. What didn't. Tomorrow's plan. Anyone hurting. Anyone's bike making a new sound. Anyone needs a half-day off.

The group that debriefs gets stronger over the trip. The group that doesn't fragments quietly, then loudly, usually around day four.

§ Closing

The convoy is the contract.

None of these rules are about the road. They're about the agreement you make with the people you're riding with — that you will ride at their pace when it's slower than yours, that you will wait when waiting costs you, that you will trade ego for the group.

Most groups never write this contract down. The good ones do, sometimes literally — on a piece of paper folded into a tank bag. We made an app that handles a few of these — convoy view, SOS, location, the glovebox — but the rules above don't need an app. They need a group that agrees.

If you want to try Broad on your next ride, we'd be glad to have you. If not, the rules above are free. Tape them to the tank bag.
