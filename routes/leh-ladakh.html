Route — 01

Leh — Ladakh.

The road that owes you nothing. Eight passes above 4,500 metres, a river you'll cross twenty-three times, and towns where the petrol pump is also the chai stop, the mechanic, and the only place to sleep.

Distance1,000+ km Duration9 days LevelAdvanced WindowJun — Sep Max altitude5,359 m · Khardung La

Leh-Ladakh is not a route. It is a pilgrimage that happens to be mapped. Every rider who has done it remembers the first time the air gets thin. Every rider who has done it has a story about something going wrong — a snapped clutch cable, a 3am hailstorm, a friend who couldn't keep food down past Sarchu.

This guide is the version we wish someone had taped to a tank bag in 2021. It is not a brochure. It will not tell you Ladakh is "magical." It will tell you when to leave, what breaks, and where the gaps are.

§ 01 — The spine

Nine days, the Manali side.

The classical loop runs Manali → Leh → Srinagar, exiting the mountains over the slightly easier Zoji La. You can ride it the other way — many do — but the gradient of the Manali side acclimatises you better than the gentler Srinagar approach. Doing it backwards is faster but harder on your body.

§ 02 — When the window opens

You have three months, and they aren't equal.

June

Atal Tunnel makes the Manali side rideable from late May, but the upper passes — Baralacha, Tanglang — only properly clear in the second week of June. Early-June riders hit late snow and unpredictable river crossings. Pretty if you're patient.

July

Monsoon hits the Manali-Leh highway. Landslides are common between Patseo and Pang. The Srinagar side is drier and safer this month — if you're flexible on direction, July is a Srinagar-in, Manali-out month.

August

The classic month. Long days, passes mostly clear, infrastructure fully open. Expect crowds. Book Pangong stays in advance.

September

The best month, in our opinion. Crowds thin, skies clear, temperatures still manageable. Snow risk creeps back by the last week — keep a buffer day for weather.

October

The closing window. Fly in, ride out before the BRO closes the highway. Do not start a Leh-Manali ride after October 5 without a satellite communicator.

§ 03 — Permits & paper

Bring the originals. Bring the copies.

Indian nationals can self-apply for the Inner Line Permit (ILP) for restricted regions — Nubra, Pangong, Tso Moriri, Hanle — at the DC office in Leh, or online via the Ladakh administration portal. Foreigners need the Protected Area Permit (PAP), which must go through a registered tour operator. Both are checked. Don't fake it.

You'll be asked for: driving licence, registration certificate, third-party insurance, PUC, and Aadhaar (Indian) or passport (foreign). If you've added these to Broad's Glovebox, they're available offline at every checkpost — including the ones with no signal.

§ 04 — What goes wrong

The three things that actually end rides.

  • AMSAcute Mountain Sickness. Headache that won't quit, nausea, breathlessness at rest. Drop altitude at the first signs — riding up makes it worse, riding down makes it better. Diamox is preventative, not curative.
  • Fuel gapThe 365 km between Tandi and Karu has no reliable petrol. Carry 10 litres extra. Sissu and Pang sometimes have black-market fuel at 2× rate.
  • MechanicalTubeless conversion before you leave. Carry: spare tubes, clutch cable, chain link, brake pads, spark plug, basic spanners. The mechanic at Pang exists. He is not a guarantee.
Ladakh doesn't punish you for being unprepared. It just stops cooperating.

Convoy auto-cluster

When you cross Baralacha La, Broad tightens the recommended convoy spread. The reason is uncomplicated: at altitude, your slowest rider becomes the group's pace. The app reminds the front not to disappear over the next switchback.

Recommended convoy size

Three to six riders. Anything larger fragments at altitude and becomes harder to keep tight. If you're a group of eight or more, we suggest splitting into two convoys with a shared trip plan.

§ 06 — Honest opinions

The motorcycle question.

Royal Enfield Himalayan is the Ladakh default for a reason — service network, parts availability, ground clearance. The Honda CB500X works. The KTM 390 Adventure works. A scooter has been ridden up Khardung La and we don't recommend it but we don't rule it out either.

Bring a bike you've ridden for at least 1,500 km this year. Ladakh is not the place to find out your suspension setup is wrong.
