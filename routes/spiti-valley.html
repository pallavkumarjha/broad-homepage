Route — 02

Spiti / Valley.

A high-altitude desert where the rivers run blue from glacier silt and the monasteries are older than most countries. Loop it from Shimla, exit through Manali — or do it backwards if you want the climb gradual.

Distance750 km Duration7 days LevelIntermediate WindowMay — Oct Max altitude4,587 m · Kunzum La

Spiti is what people picture when they say "Tibet" but cannot afford. It is a Buddhist valley wedged between Kinnaur and Ladakh, populated by villages that have stood since before the Mughals, watered by rivers the colour of cobalt glass.

It rides easier than Ladakh and lies harder. The altitude is lower (mostly under 4,000 m), the road condition is mixed (some patches that could pass for runway, others that have not been graded in a decade), and the population density is approximately one yak per square kilometre. You do not get bored on this route. You get astonished, then exhausted, then both.

§ 01 — The loop

Seven days, Shimla in.

The classical Spiti loop enters from Shimla via Kinnaur, climbs gradually through Sangla and Chitkul, crosses into Spiti at Khab, and exits over Kunzum La into Lahaul and onward to Manali. Doing it Manali-in is faster but harder on acclimatisation — Kunzum La throws you to 4,587 m on day one.

01
Shimla → Sangla
Via Narkanda. The Sutlej gorge tightens dramatically after Rampur. Sangla sits at 2,700 m — your first real climb. Side trip to Chitkul (last village before Tibet) if you have time.
220 km9 hrs
02
Sangla → Nako
Via Reckong Peo (last ATM until Kaza). The road tunnels through cliffs cut like cake. Nako lake is small, still, and ringed by a 12th-century monastery.
120 km5 hrs
03
Nako → Tabo → Dhankar
Tabo's monastery is 1,000 years old, walls covered in Indo-Tibetan murals. Dhankar fort sits on a knife-edge ridge and you can hike to a glacial lake above it. Easy day, big payoff.
100 km4 hrs
04
Dhankar → Kaza
Short hop. Kaza is Spiti's biggest town — and a town here means three streets and one petrol pump. Spend the afternoon at Key Monastery. This is your base for the next two nights.
40 km1.5 hrs
05
Kaza loop — Kibber, Komik, Hikkim, Langza
The "highest villages" day. Komik claims the world's highest motorable village (4,587 m). Hikkim has the highest post office (4,400 m — buy postcards). Langza has Buddha statues and fossils older than the mountains.
80 km5 hrs
06
Kaza → Chandratal
Over Kunzum La (4,587 m). The road from here to Chandratal is the worst stretch of the loop — broken tarmac, river crossings, no signal. Chandratal itself is camp-only. The lake is what every photo says it is.
100 km5 hrs
07
Chandratal → Manali
Down to Batal, on to Gramphu, through Atal Tunnel, into Manali. The descent feels like decompression — vegetation returns, signal returns, the air thickens.

§ 02 — Why October

The last good window.

Spiti's official season runs May through October, but the months are not equal. Most riders do it in July and August because that's when school holidays land and Instagram does the rest of the marketing. We disagree. Here's the case for October.

The summer crowd is gone

Homestays are half-empty. Kaza's main street is walkable. The yak-and-traveller ratio swings back in favour of yaks. You can park outside Key Monastery, not three switchbacks below it.

Skies are at peak clarity

Post-monsoon dust settles. The valley's geology — layered, alien, deeply old — reveals itself in a way it doesn't through July's haze. Photographers know this; ride photographers post in October but shoot it earlier. We're telling you the secret.

Harvest festivals

Late September and early October are festival weeks in many monasteries. Cham dances, masked rituals, butter tea you'll be served whether you want it or not. The valley becomes a working community celebrating, not a tourist set.

Snow risk arrives by November

Don't push past the third week. Kunzum La closes when it closes, and BRO doesn't text first. October 25 is the latest comfortable date for the Manali exit. After that, you're flying out from Bhuntar or returning the way you came.

§ 03 — What Spiti demands

The three non-negotiables.

  • AcclimatisationDon't fly to Bhuntar and ride straight into Spiti via Manali. The Shimla approach exists because it climbs you up gradually — Sangla, Nako, Tabo each add about 500m. Skip steps and your body files a complaint at Kunzum.
  • FuelTwo full tanks of buffer between Reckong Peo and Kaza. Kaza's pump is unreliable — sometimes dry for a day, sometimes selling at 2× rate. Carry 5–10 litres in a jerrycan and you'll never use it. Don't, and you'll need it.
  • CashATMs in Reckong Peo and Kaza only — and Kaza's go offline more days than they're up. Carry enough cash for the entire valley. Homestays don't run UPI when the network is down, which is most of the time.

§ 04 — The motorcycle question

Almost anything will do.

Spiti is more forgiving than Ladakh on bike choice. Royal Enfield Classic, Himalayan, KTM Duke 390, Honda CB350 — all have done the loop without drama. The Kunzum La descent into Batal is where suspension matters; the rest is paved or gravel.

What does matter: a tubeless setup or a spare tube, a working horn (you'll use it), and a windscreen if you don't want the cold to be a feature for seven days.

§ 05 — The Broad notes

Spiti, downloaded.

Spiti is a connectivity desert. What works on the homepage demo doesn't matter here — what matters is what works without bars. We tested the entire loop in October 2024 with one phone permanently in airplane mode.

The Spiti tile pack

450 MB. Covers Shimla → Manali via the entire loop, plus Chitkul, Pin Valley, and the upper villages. Download once. We update it twice a year.

Documents at every checkpost

You'll be asked for ID at Sumdo (entry into Spiti from Kinnaur) and again at Losar (exit into Lahaul). Broad's Glovebox holds these offline, encrypted. Hand the constable your phone, not your printout.

Convoy size: keep it small

Two to four riders. Spiti's villages have limited homestay capacity — booking eight beds three days in advance is harder than it sounds. Smaller convoys also mean a smaller footprint in a valley that's still figuring out what tourism does to it.

SOS in the gorges

The Spiti river gorge between Nako and Tabo has long signal-blackout sections. Trigger Broad's SOS in those zones and it queues the message. The next signal — usually somewhere near Sumdo — fires it through.

§ 06 — One more thing

Spiti deserves better tourists.

The valley's ecosystem is old and fragile. The villages are working communities, not photo backdrops. Carry your trash out. Don't haggle at homestays — the difference is twenty rupees to you and a meal to them. Don't fly drones over monasteries during prayer hours. Don't ride into the rivers.

This is the kind of advice that sounds preachy when written down. Read it anyway.
